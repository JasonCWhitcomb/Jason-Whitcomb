04
Three things I'd want to explore with you

Not a plan yet. Directions worth the room.

i.

Give impact the same measurement spine that pipeline gets.

The framework I built for a $20M+ commercial portfolio — investment tiers, ROI standards, executive reporting — transfers almost directly to the Strategic Advisory Board and the .org event portfolio. The result: a board-ready impact narrative that keeps the story and adds the proof, so the value that's always been hard to quantify finally has a number beside it.

ii.

Prove "do more with less" on our own operations first.

You've raised the question every nonprofit is now living — how do you grow impact as staffing shrinks? I'd make ServiceNow.org's own advisory and event operations the flagship reference case: run them on agentic AI, measure what it frees, then package it as a playbook nonprofits can adopt. We'd offer a result we've lived, not a slide.

iii.

Turn the room into the pipeline.

I sit at the seam between ServiceNow's commercial event world — Knowledge, the AI Summits, the World Forums — and the mission. There are tens of thousands of customers in those rooms who already say they share our purpose. I'd engineer moments that convert that goodwill into commitments — nonprofit partners signed, grant applicants sourced, RiseUp mentors enlisted — bridging your two ambitions, storytelling and social impact, into one motion.

05
Why me — the real reason

Fifteen years building the moment. Now I want to build a legacy of impact.

There's a moment I know better than almost anything: the house lights fall, the room leans in, and someone I've spent months preparing walks into the light. It's the best feeling in my work — and I've always watched it from the wings, in the dark, which is where the person who builds the moment stands.

For a long time that was enough. And then, quietly, it wasn't. I realized I didn't want the applause. I wanted the moment to land somewhere that mattered — to be the reason a mission moved, not just the reason a stage looked good.

That pull isn't new. At Cisco I spent years building inclusion and community partnerships, much of it alongside nonprofits — the same craft, aimed at who gets access instead of who gets attention. It's why I built Kinetic Culture. Underneath all of it was one stubborn question: when the room emptied, whose life was actually different?

So here I am, reaching for the legacy instead of the applause. Sending this without knowing how it lands is uncomfortable — the safe move is to stay backstage, where nothing can be turned down. But the work you care about most is the work you have to be willing to be seen wanting. So I'm letting myself be seen wanting this.

Not because it's the next rung on a ladder. Because for once, the room I want to help build is one where the whole point is someone else's life getting better. That's worth walking into the light for.

— Jason
